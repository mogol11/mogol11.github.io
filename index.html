Wikipedia Redesign

Link to project

The goal of this project was to redesign a Wikipedia article in a way that creates a better reading experience for the reader. Some of the ways I feel I've accomplished this are by increasing the line height (the amout of space between lines) increasing the font size, and decreasing the line length (the amound of horizontal space a line takes up.) I also moved the table of contents to the side, and made it scroll with the page, so that it's always visible, and hid the links that not many users would click, like "page information" within dropdown menus inside the sidebar. I wrote most of the code for my redesign on my own, but I did copy a few bits of code from Wikipedia. I've annotated within the HTML code which sections are copied. The main challenge with this piece was using Javascript. As I designed my new layout, I realized that it wouldn't work unless I added Javascript (it allows the dropdowns to function.) If you take a look, there's not actually much Javascript there, but the challenge was in finding the most efficent way to handle everything that needed to be done. At one point, my Javascript code was over 100 lines, at which point I realized there was a much better way to accomplish what I wanted and rewrote it to be 23 lines, including empty lines for spacing and notes. This was especially valuable because I was able to apply a lot of the same ideas to this portfolio.

Shakespeare's Sonnet

Link to assignment

For this assignment, I randomly chose an adjective and had to use only visual design to make Shakespeare's Sonnet #18 "feel" like that adjective to the reader. I wan't allowed to change the content of the sonnet in any way. My adjective was "annoyed," so the first thing I did was look up a color that's described as "annoyed." I found a website that said orange is an "annoyed" color, so I made my text orange. I chose the font for the header because the little lines sticking out made it seem like an annoyed person with a shaking hand was writing it. The font that the main body of text is written in made me think of a fat, annoyed businessman writing an annoyed letter, so I also used a papery background to go with that feeling. Font Pairings

Link to assignment

For this assignment, I had to create three pairings of one header font and one main body font. Each pairing was supposed to have a different feeling to it. I think the three pairings I chose were each able to create a different feel, even though there was no actual content.

Visual Hierarchy

http://store.steampowered.com/

This website demonstrates good visual hierarchy, since the first thing your eyes are pulled to is the featured deals (the large Ubisoft banner) next, your eyes are drawn to a scrolling list of other featured deals just below this, followed by a spotlight to the right of this. This is due to the sizes of the images relative to each other and their positioning on the page.


http://www.mrbottles.com/

This website demonstrates bad web design, because your eyes are drawn everywhere at once, and it's hard to tell what you should be focusing on. You've got the colorful picture of the bottles at the top of the page, but then you've got the red text that pops out among the (barely legible) blue text. As soon as the slideshow changes, your eyes are drawn there too. But the worst (read: best) part of the website is that when you load the page, good old Mr. Bottles himself pops out of nowhere and starts going on about his bottle collection (an impressive one, may I add,) and shows off his impressive magic skills by summoning bottles out of nowhere. Not only do the sound and movement immediately distract you from whatever else you may have been focused on, but Mr. Bottles seems to have decided his lecture is more important than the actual content of the page, seeing as he covers up the text and images in the bottom left corner, which you can't read again until he's done talking about his magnificent bottle collection. On the plus side, this website did make me laugh.

Critique of Max's Portfolio

Link

I wasn't really sure how long this was supposed to be, so I just wrote my thoughts about each page of the portfolio.

The first things on the front page that catch my eye are the colorful words that say "Max Strome." I think that this is where the eye should be drawn first, and I like the font and colors used, but looking at the page as a whole, I feel like maybe these words should be a little bigger; it just feels a little too small since it's actually smaller than one section of the drop-down menu. On the "Code" page, I like the two lines that help draw your attention to the center of the page, though I feel like you may want to add a large <h1> to the top of the page; to me, not having some sort of large text or title at the top of the page feels like there's something missing. I also feel like you should make the links on this page (only slightly) larger, since I have a hard time distinguishing the links from the descriptions at first glance.

The "Me" link under "Bio" doesn't work at school (it's blocked,) and it puts you through a redirect notice before you can access it. This isn't actually a problem with your website's design, but I still feel like you should fix it. On the other hand, I really like the "Bio" page under the "Bio" link. The only criticism I can think of is that you might have a little too much empty space around the text within the box, which you could probably solve by making the text a little bit bigger, or maybe even filling up the empty space with pictures.

Overall, I really like the design of this portfolio; there's just a few small things that I think you might want to change around.


Critique of Jess' Portfolio

Link

On the welcome page, I like the bright blue background and the dark blue you used for your name, because they seem to fit with the feel of the website that the font helps create. In class, some people mentioned removing the overline and underline from the Welcome link. I don't think this is a bad idea, but if you want to keep the lines, I think it would look good if you made the overline and underline the same distances from the text. Moving on to the next page, I like the use of the roses as bullet points, since they contrast nicely with the background and stand out well. My recommendations for this page are that you make the links a little bigger, since they feel kind of small compared to everything else, and center the two links, since it feels like there's space for another link over to the right.

You said in class you're not done with your Tumblr section, so I'll skip that and move onto the Codepen section. I like the way the links are organized here, but they feel too close to the top of the screen for me. Subtle Patterns Critique

Link

I've used this website a lot for background images for my websites, so I figured it might make sense to write my first analysis about it.

When I first open the website, the first thing I see is the box advertising the Photoshop plugin, since the background of the page is very light and this box is dark. It also helps that it's so high up on the page. As someone who doesn't use Photoshop, (MS Paint ftw) this annoys me a little, but at least it's nice looking, unlike some online ads I've seen. Though I personally don't care about the plugin, it makes a lot of sense for them to make this the first thing to catch the viewer's eye, since they get $12 whenever someone buys the plugin.

One of my favorite features of this website is that you can click any pattern and it will become the background image for the website. This allows you to see how the pattern will look as a background for a page without downloading it and linking it in your CSS, which, for me, has been very useful. This is kind of a nitpicky complaint, but I feel like there are just a few too many patterns listed on one page. I just feel like I'm scrolling a little bit too far to reach the bottom of the page, and I would prefer it if there were either less patterns per page, or if the page was infinitely scrolling, because at least that way I wouldn't be expecting to reach the bottom.

The navigation bar at the top is easily visible, but not too obtrusive. It's not difficult to read any of the words on it, even though the darkest color in it is a fairly light green. I like that rather than making a button that says "home" in the nav bar, they used the website's symbol and made it green, because this makes it stand out more from the rest of the buttons on the bar. I like how the bar scrolls with the page so it's easy to click any of the links on it at any time or search for a pattern. Speaking of searching, I've used the search bar a few times and haven't actually found it that useful, but I suspect that's more a problem with the names of the patterns than anything, since they're not always accurately named. (The "Skulls" pattern lacks even a single skull.) I also like how the nav bar is only slightly transparent. It feels more like a cloudy glass window than just a bar sitting at the top of the page, because you can see just the outlines of boxes behind it, but not much more than that.

One thing I've noticed that annoys me is that there's this mysterious white box that appears in the lower right-hand corner of the page, and it doesn't seem to do anything. I don't know if this is a problem with the page itself or if it might have something to do with my monitor's fairly small resolution (1366 x 768) but regardless of the source of the problem, it should probably be fixed, since if it's got to do with my monitor size, that's a problem with the fluidity of the site, and if it doesn't, then it's still a problem.

Speaking of fluidity, this website handles it okay in my opinion. The page just gives up empty space around the content as you shrink it, at least until it gets smaller than about 1000 pixels. After that, you just get a horizontal scroll bar. This is definitely not the best way to handle small window sizes in my opinion. Those previews of patterns are pretty big, and while they look nice when you have the browser space for them, I doubt anyone would be upset if they got a little smaller when the browser gets smaller for the sake of fluidity.

I also looked at the website using iPhone 5 emulation, and there doesn't seem to be a mobile version. When you think about it, this does kind of make sense, given the purpose of the website (you probably don't decide on backgrounds for your websites using your 640 pixel wide phone,) but if one did want to use the website on their phone, it wouldn't really be possible. The navigation bar cuts off halfway through the word "About," and due to its fixed positioning, scrolling horizontally doesn't scroll the bar, meaning the search bar isn't accessible. The ad on the page also blocks the descriptions of the patterns, so it's hard to read about or look at them. (I think a real iPhone might display this ad differently, however.)

The font used on the page is a simple sans-serif font (Open Sans) and it's easily readable. It fits well with the somewhat minimalistic style of the page.

All in all, I like the design of the Subtle Patterns website. My main problem with it is that it lacks much fluidity, especially since it doesn't seem like a particularly difficult problem to fix (just shrink the preview patterns,) but it's got a lot of nice features too. I love how you can click a pattern to preview it, as well as the font choice. Subtle Patterns is a nice looking website so long as you keep your horizontal browser size above 1000 pixels.
